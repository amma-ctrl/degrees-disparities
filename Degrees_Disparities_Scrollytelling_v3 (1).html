Degrees & Disparities

They earned the degrees. The leadership seats stayed empty.

Black women in the United States have made remarkable progress in educational attainment, earning Master's, Professional, and Doctoral degrees at rising rates. Yet across Corporate America, Healthcare, Government, and Academia, they remain significantly underrepresented in leadership — even when they hold the same qualifications as their peers.

An analysis of 372,000+ records from the 2023 American Community Survey examines whether this gap is real, how large it is, and which industries are the worst offenders.

Data: 2023 ACS IPUMS · U.S. Census Bureau · 372,226 observations
01 · Introduction

Despite educational success, Black women remain significantly underrepresented in leadership

Research suggests that Black women encounter multiple layers of discrimination, including racial and gender biases, exclusion from networks, and higher expectations in leadership (Crenshaw, 1989). This intersectionality creates unique challenges, often limiting access to positions of power and influence despite academic qualifications.

The lack of diversity in leadership positions is not just a matter of numbers, but reflects deep-rooted structural inequalities and biases that persist in workplace cultures.

Research Question

Is there a statistically significant disparity between the proportion of Black women who earn advanced degrees and their representation in higher leadership positions across various industries?

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

There is no statistically significant gap between the proportion of Black women earning graduate degrees and their representation in higher leadership positions.

Alternate Hypothesis (H₁)

There is a statistically significant gap between the proportion of Black women earning graduate degrees and their lower rates of representation in higher leadership positions.

02 · Methodology

From census data to statistical evidence

The analysis draws on the 2023 American Community Survey (ACS) via IPUMS, focusing on individuals with advanced degrees (Master's, Professional, Doctoral). Key variables include education level, leadership status, industry sector, and race/gender.

01
Data Preparation
Created binary variables for Black women with advanced degrees and leadership roles. Mapped ~500 OCC2010 occupation codes into 4 industry sectors and a leadership classifier using the Census occupation code breakdown.
02
Descriptive Analysis
Calculated leadership proportions by group (Black Women vs. All Other Races & Genders) overall and within each industry. Visualized comparisons with grouped bar charts on Tableau Public.
03
Statistical Testing
Chi-square tests assessed association between race/gender and leadership. A Logistic Regression Model estimated the probability of holding a leadership position, controlling for group and industry.

07 · Conclusion

The degrees are earned. The doors remain closed.

Overall, the results across all analyses support rejecting the null hypothesis (H₀) and accepting the alternate hypothesis (H₁). There is a statistically significant gap between the proportion of Black women earning advanced degrees and their representation in leadership positions across industries.

These findings are highly relevant in highlighting the persistent structural and systemic barriers that hinder the career progression of Black women, despite their educational success. The disparities revealed across industries suggest that industry-specific factors, such as workplace culture, implicit bias, and exclusion from informal networks, continue to affect leadership representation.

However, the study is not without its limitations. The data used is the 2023 ACS IPUMS Data, which may not fully capture the nuances of career progression, such as work experience or professional networks. Additionally, the logistic regression assumes a specific functional form that may not fully capture the complexity of this relationship.

Despite these limitations, the findings are robust and persuasive. Addressing these barriers would not only create more equitable opportunities for Black women but also lead to more diverse and inclusive leadership across all sectors.
