Degrees & Disparities · An Empirical Analysis

They earned the degrees. The leadership seats stayed empty.

Black women in the United States have made remarkable progress in educational attainment, earning Master's, Professional, and Doctoral degrees at rising rates. Yet across Corporate America, Healthcare, Government, and Academia, they remain significantly underrepresented in leadership — even when they hold the same qualifications as their peers.

An analysis of 372,000+ records from the 2023 American Community Survey examines whether this gap is real, how large it is, and which industries are the worst offenders.

Data: 2023 ACS IPUMS · U.S. Census Bureau · 372,226 observations
01 · Introduction

Despite educational success, Black women remain significantly underrepresented in leadership

Research suggests that Black women encounter multiple layers of discrimination, including racial and gender biases, exclusion from networks, and higher expectations in leadership (Crenshaw, 1989). This intersectionality creates unique challenges, often limiting access to positions of power and influence despite academic qualifications.

The lack of diversity in leadership positions is not just a matter of numbers, but reflects deep-rooted structural inequalities and biases that persist in workplace cultures across every sector of the American economy.

Research Question

Is there a statistically significant disparity between the proportion of Black women who earn advanced degrees and their representation in higher leadership positions across various industries?

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

There is no statistically significant gap between the proportion of Black women earning graduate degrees and their representation in higher leadership positions.

Alternate Hypothesis (H₁)

There is a statistically significant gap between the proportion of Black women earning graduate degrees and their lower rates of representation in higher leadership positions.

02 · Methodology

From census microdata to statistical evidence

The analysis draws on the 2023 American Community Survey (ACS) via IPUMS, focusing on individuals with advanced degrees (Master's, Professional, Doctoral). Key variables include education level, leadership status, industry sector, and race/gender. Hover over a step to learn more, or click to expand details.

Descriptive Analysis
Calculated leadership proportions by group overall and within each industry. Created grouped bar charts and proportion tables.
→
04
Chi-Square Testing
Ran Pearson chi-square tests of independence, stratified by industry. Examined standardized residuals for per-cell significance.
→
05
Logistic Regression
Estimated binary logistic model: P(leadership=1) ~ group + industry. Reported odds ratios with 95% CIs to isolate the Black women effect.
→

Validation & Reporting
Checked model diagnostics, residual patterns, and assumption violations. Produced visualizations in R and Tableau Public.

About this method

What it is. A Pearson chi-square residual plot. Residuals are (observed − expected) / √expected. Above ±2 is significant; ±5 or more is large.

Why use it. A single statistic (χ² = 4,217.3, p < 2.2e-16) confirms gaps aren't chance — residuals map where the signal lives cell by cell.

Key findings. Corporate for Black women (−12.86) and Academia for all others (+45.84) are the strongest individual contributions. Every cell exceeds the |2| threshold.

The reading is symmetric: where Black women are underrepresented in leadership (Corporate, −12.86), they are correspondingly overrepresented in non-leadership (+5.84) — the same people sorted into different rungs. Academia is the inverse trap: near-zero deviation for Black women masks a massive surplus of other groups in leadership (+45.84).

An odds ratio of 0.707 means Black women have approximately 29.3% lower odds of holding a leadership position compared to all other groups, even after controlling for industry sector. The effect is highly significant (p < 0.001).

Logistic Regression · glm(leadership ~ group + industry, family=binomial) · N = 372,226
About this method

What it is. A logistic regression models the log-odds of a binary outcome as a linear combination of predictors. Coefficients are exponentiated into odds ratios: below 1 = reduced odds, above 1 = increased odds.

Why use it. The earlier analyses establish that a gap exists; this isolates how much is attributable to being a Black woman specifically, separate from sector composition.

Significance. The 0.707 OR (p < 0.001) is a within-industry effect: comparing Black women to peers in the same sector, the leadership shortfall remains.

07 · Conclusion

The degrees are earned. The doors remain closed.

The results across all analyses are consistent and statistically robust. The evidence below shows which hypothesis the data supports:

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

There is no statistically significant gap between the proportion of Black women earning graduate degrees and their representation in higher leadership positions.

✗ Rejected
Alternate Hypothesis (H₁)

There is a statistically significant gap between the proportion of Black women earning graduate degrees and their lower rates of representation in higher leadership positions.

✓ Accepted

These findings highlight persistent structural and systemic barriers that hinder the career progression of Black women despite their educational success. Industry-specific factors — workplace culture, implicit bias, exclusion from informal networks, and differential access to sponsorship — continue to affect leadership representation in ways that credentials alone cannot overcome.

However, the study is not without limitations. The 2023 ACS IPUMS data may not fully capture career progression nuances such as work experience or professional networks. The logistic regression also assumes a functional form that may not capture the full complexity of this relationship.

Despite these limitations, the findings are robust and persuasive. Chi-square test statistics (χ² = 4,217.3, p < 2.2e-16), standardized residuals exceeding |12| in Corporate, and an odds ratio of 0.707 (p < 0.001) all converge on the same conclusion. Addressing these barriers would create more equitable opportunities for Black women and more diverse leadership across all sectors.
