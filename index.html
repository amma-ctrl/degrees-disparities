Degrees & Disparities · An Empirical Analysis

They earned the degrees. The leadership seats stayed empty.

Black women in the United States have made remarkable progress in educational attainment, earning Master's, Professional, and Doctoral degrees at rising rates. Yet across Corporate America, Healthcare, Government, and Academia, they remain significantly underrepresented in leadership — even when they hold the same qualifications as their peers.

An analysis of 372,000+ records from the 2023 American Community Survey examines whether this gap is real, how large it is, and which industries are the worst offenders.

Data: 2023 ACS IPUMS · U.S. Census Bureau · 372,226 observations
01 · Introduction

The credentials are there. So why aren't the leadership seats?

As a Black woman currently earning a graduate degree, I kept noticing a pattern in research and in life: highly educated Black women rarely ended up in the leadership roles their credentials should have unlocked. Previous studies had found this in academic settings — that Black women remain underrepresented in tenured faculty and administrative leadership even as their share of doctoral degrees rises (Turner et al., 2008). I wanted to know if this pattern held outside academia, too — in Corporate America, Healthcare, and the Public Sector.

What is intersectionality?

In 1989, legal scholar Kimberlé Crenshaw coined the term intersectionality to describe how Black women experience discrimination that is neither purely about race nor purely about gender, but a compound of both. A Black woman walks into a workplace as both Black and a woman — and the barriers she faces are shaped by that combined identity, not by either factor alone.

This is why the analysis can't stop at "are Black women underrepresented in leadership?" It has to ask two follow-up questions:

1. Is the gap a gender effect? If so, Black men would face a similar shortfall. So we compare Black women to Black men — holding race constant, varying sex.
2. Is the gap a race effect? If so, women of all races would face it equally. So we compare Black women to women of other races — holding sex constant, varying race.

If Black women lag both Black men and non-Black women, the disparity is intersectional — it lives at the intersection of the two identities, not in either one alone. That's the framework this report follows.

Research Question

Is there a statistically significant disparity between the proportion of Black women who earn advanced degrees and their representation in higher leadership positions across U.S. industries?

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

There is no statistically significant gap between the proportion of Black women earning graduate degrees and their representation in higher leadership positions.

Alternate Hypothesis (H₁)

There is a statistically significant gap between the proportion of Black women earning graduate degrees and their lower rates of representation in higher leadership positions.

02 · Methodology

From census microdata to statistical evidence

The analysis draws on the 2023 American Community Survey (ACS) via IPUMS, focusing on individuals with advanced degrees (Master's, Professional, Doctoral). 03 · The Leadership Gap

One group falls farther below the line than any other

A first look at the data, broken out by race and gender. Each bar shows how a group's share of leadership compares to its share of the overall advanced-degree workforce. If a group earned and led in equal proportion, the bar would sit at zero. A bar above zero means that group is overrepresented in leadership; a bar below zero means they are underrepresented.

Looking across the twelve race-by-gender groups, a pattern emerges: every group of men sits at or above the line — most overrepresented in leadership — while every group of women sits below it. Black women fall the farthest, at −11.4 percentage points, deeper than any other group on the chart. White women, the next-lowest, sit at −8.4. The 3-point jump from White women to Black women is the single largest step on the chart, and the first hint that the disparity Black women face is more than just a gender effect.

04 · Industry Breakdown

The size of the gap changes dramatically by industry

An aggregate gap of −11.4 pp tells us something is happening, but it doesn't tell us where. Different industries operate under very different rules — civil service systems, professional licensure, tenure tracks, market hiring. To understand whether the disparity is concentrated or universal, the analysis breaks every advanced-degree holder into one of four sector groups: Corporate/Private, Healthcare, Public Sector & Legal, and Academia & Research.

Below, the leadership share for Black women is compared against the leadership share for everyone else within each sector. The gap shrinks to under 2 percentage points in Corporate and Academia, but yawns open to 19–22 percentage points in Public Sector and Healthcare. Corporate / Private Sector

Corporate has the second-smallest gap of the four sectors. Black women hold leadership at 21.6%, against 23.3% for everyone else — a 1.7 percentage-point shortfall. The gap looks modest in isolation, but Corporate is by far the largest employer of advanced-degree holders in the U.S., so even a small percentage gap translates to many thousands of missing leaders. And as the intersectionality charts below will show, the small headline gap conceals a much wider divide once we look within Black workers and across women of different races.

Healthcare

Healthcare shows the largest gap of any sector. Among advanced-degree holders in the field, 42.0% of "all others" hold leadership roles — but only 20.3% of Black women do. That's a 21.7 percentage-point gap, more than double Black women's own leadership share. In a sector where Black women earn graduate degrees at high rates (often as nurses, public-health professionals, and clinicians), the structural drop-off into senior medical and administrative leadership is dramatic.

Public Sector & Legal

Public Sector and Legal — the home of civil service, government, and the courts — shows the second-largest gap of the four sectors. 05 · Chi-Square Residuals

Statistical testing confirms: the gaps are not due to chance

The bar charts above describe what the disparities look like. A chi-square test answers a different question: are these disparities statistically real, or could they have arisen by chance? The overall test returns χ² = 4,217.3, p < 2.2e-16 — vanishingly small. We can reject the idea that the gaps are random noise. But the single statistic doesn't tell us where the deviations are largest. For that, we look at the standardized residuals — one number per cell of the table below.

Each cell measures how far the observed count deviates from what we'd expect if leadership were unrelated to race and gender. Violet squares mean fewer people than expected sit in that combination; teal squares mean more. Color intensity scales with magnitude — anything beyond ±2 is significant, beyond ±5 is large.

The story the residuals tell is symmetric. Black women are sharply underrepresented in Corporate leadership (−12.9) and Healthcare leadership (−10.0) — and correspondingly overrepresented in those sectors' non-leadership ranks (+8.7 and +6.8). The same Black women, sorted into different rungs. Meanwhile, "all other groups" show the inverse pattern: they're overrepresented in Healthcare leadership (+42.9), Public Sector leadership (+45.8), and Academia leadership (+6.5), while being underrepresented in Corporate leadership (−51.8). Every cell exceeds the ±2 threshold, meaning nothing in this table is consistent with chance.

About this method

A Pearson chi-square residual plot. Each cell's value is (observed − expected) / √expected, where "expected" is the count we'd see if leadership status were independent of group identity. By convention, residuals beyond ±2 are statistically significant, and residuals beyond ±5 are considered large.

This method was chosen because the overall chi-square statistic only confirms that a relationship exists; the residual grid shows where that relationship is concentrated, cell by cell, so the reader can locate which specific group-by-sector combinations drive the overall result.

06 · Logistic Regression

A logistic model quantifies the odds: being a Black woman reduces the odds of leadership

The chi-square test showed the disparities are real and significant — but it can't quantify how much being a Black woman, specifically, lowers the odds of leadership. A logistic regression can. The model below estimates the probability of holding a leadership role as a function of two predictors: whether the person is a Black woman, and which industry sector they work in. Sector is included as a control because Black women aren't evenly distributed across industries; we want the Black-woman effect after the sector composition has been accounted for.

The headline coefficient is −0.346 on the Black-women indicator. Exponentiated, that becomes an odds ratio of 0.707 — meaning Black women's odds of holding leadership are about 70.7% of the odds for everyone else with the same credentials, working in the same sector. That's a 29.3% reduction in odds, p < 0.001. The effect is large and tightly estimated.

Reading down the table: the intercept tells us the baseline odds for the reference group (non-Black-women, in the reference industry). The Black-women row is the effect we're after — a 29.3% reduction in leadership odds, even after sector is controlled for. The industry rows are sector effects on top of that baseline: Healthcare and Public Sector both have higher leadership rates than the reference industry (ORs of 1.47 and 1.69), while Corporate has lower (0.64). But the Black-women effect is independent of those — it shows up regardless of which sector someone works in.

About this method

A binary logistic regression. The model treats "holds a leadership role" as a 0/1 outcome and estimates the log-odds as a linear function of predictors. Each coefficient is exponentiated into an odds ratio: values below 1 represent reduced odds, values above 1 represent increased odds, and 1.0 means no effect.

Logistic regression was chosen over a Linear Probability Model because the outcome is binary; the logistic link function constrains predicted probabilities to [0, 1] and yields a more honest treatment of the data. Industry is included as a control variable so the Black-women coefficient measures the effect of group identity net of sector composition, not confounded with it.

07 · Conclusion

The degrees are earned. The doors remain closed.

Across every analysis in this report, the same finding emerges. The diverging chart placed Black women 11.4 percentage points below the population average — farther than any other race-by-gender group. The industry breakdown revealed gaps of 19–22 percentage points in Public Sector and Healthcare, and showed that even the "smallest" gaps in Corporate and Academia (under 2 pp) conceal much larger intersectional disparities once you look within Black workers and across women by race. The chi-square residuals demonstrated that none of these patterns are consistent with chance. And the logistic regression quantified the cost of being a Black woman, specifically: −29.3% lower odds of holding leadership, even after controlling for sector.

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

There is no statistically significant gap between the proportion of Black women earning graduate degrees and their representation in higher leadership positions.

✗ Rejected
Alternate Hypothesis (H₁)

There is a statistically significant gap between the proportion of Black women earning graduate degrees and their lower rates of representation in higher leadership positions.

✓ Accepted

These results matter because they speak to the disconnect between educational attainment and access to power. Black women have made enormous gains in graduate education over the past two decades, yet their share of leadership has barely moved. The intersectional framework helps explain why: the disparity isn't reducible to a "gender problem" (Black men face their own underrepresentation, but a different one) or a "race problem" (women of other races also face shortfalls, but not as steep). It lives at the intersection — in the specific compound of being Black and being a woman in American workplaces. Workplace culture, sponsorship networks, informal access to power, and implicit bias all operate together on that combined identity in ways credentials cannot override.

The study has limitations worth naming. The ACS records sex as a binary and doesn't capture non-binary identities. Leadership is inferred from occupation codes, which misses internal corporate hierarchies (a "manager" code could mean a team lead or a senior VP). The data is cross-sectional, so it can't show whether the gap is widening or narrowing over time. And the logistic regression assumes a particular functional form that, while standard, may not capture all the complexity of how race, gender, and sector interact.

Despite these limitations, the convergence of evidence — diverging chart, industry breakdowns, intersectionality charts, χ² = 4,217.3 with p < 2.2e-16, residuals exceeding |12| in Corporate, an odds ratio of 0.707 — is striking. The disparity is not in question. The question now is what to do about it. Closing this gap would not only create more equitable opportunities for Black women; it would produce more diverse leadership across every sector of the American economy.
